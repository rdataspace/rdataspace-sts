The U.S. Space Shuttle (or Space Transport System - STS) was the world's first reusable spacecraft, and the first spacecraft that can carry large payloads both to and from orbit. The shuttle launched like a rocket, maneuvered in Earth orbit like a spacecraft and landed like an airplane.

Shuttle components included the Orbiter Vehicle (OV), a pair of recoverable Solid Rocket Boosters (SRBs), and the expendable External Tank (ET) containing liquid hydrogen and liquid oxygen.

The Shuttle was launched vertically, like a conventional rocket, with the two SRBs operating in parallel with the OV's three main engines, which were fueled from the ET. The SRBs were jettisoned before the vehicle reached orbit, and the ET was jettisoned just before orbit insertion.

The orbiter's two Orbital Maneuvering System (OMS) engines were used for orbit insertion and de-orbit to re-enter the atmosphere at the conclusion of the mission. The orbiter then glided as a space plane to a runway landing.

After landing the orbiter was flown back to the Kennedy Space Center (KSC), in Florida, on the Shuttle Carrier Aircraft, a specially modified version of the Boeing 747 passenger aircraft.

The first orbiter, Enterprise, built in 1976, had no orbital capability and was used in Approach and Landing Tests. Four fully operational orbiters were initially built: Challenger (OV-099), Columbia (OV-102), Discovery (OV-103), and Atlantis (OV-104).

Two shuttles were lost in mission accidents: Challenger in 1986 and Columbia in 2003, with a total of fourteen astronauts killed. A fifth operational orbiter, Endeavour (OV-105), was built in 1991 to replace Challenger.

Space shuttles were launched from the U.S. National Aeronautics and Space Administration (NASA) facilities at Kennedy Space Center (KSC) in Florida. Landings occurred at the KSC and Edwards Air Force Base (EAFB) in California. One landing occurred on Northrup Strip at White Sands Space Harbor in Las Cruces, New Mexico, which is the backup landing site.

There were a total of 135 missions flown by Space Shuttles from 1981 until the their retirement in 2011.

 

About This Article

Article Objective

This article is an overview of the U.S. Space Shuttle Program with technical details of the Space Transport System (STS) main components. The basis is a chronological list of Shuttle missions with the orbiter and primary payload. Details of personnel involved or the outcomes from the missions are not covered.

Throughout the article links are given to relevant external web sites with further information on a topic. Where relevant, internal links are also given to other articles on this web site.

Introduction - This page

Missions - A space shuttle 'mission' includes its launch from earth, attaining orbit, executing the mission objectives and returning to Earth. This page gives a table listing the missions of all the shuttles in chronological order. It also includes a summary of the shuttle's primary payloads.

Payloads - The shuttle could carry small payloads in the pressurised crew cabin and large single or multiple payloads in the un-pressurised payload bay. This page lists the primary payloads, arranged into the following categories. More details are given for each category in additional pages linked from the 'Payload' page.

* Shuttle Testing - A number of instrument packages were carried to test the shuttle's systems for launch and orbital flight.
* Orbital Environment Research - Used the environment of the orbiting spacecraft for microgravity and space exposure research.
* Solar System Research and Astronomy - Experiments dedicated to our Earth, sun, solar system and astronomy.
* Satellites - Over thirty long duration satellites were launched into orbit using the shuttle as a first stage.
* Space Station Support - Cargo and components delivered to the Russian space station Mir and the International Space Station.

Facilities - A number of facilities were designed to be carried in the shuttles payload bay, returned to Earth, refurbished and re-used on other missions. These included pressurised habitable and non-habitable modules as well as un-pressurised carriers.

Flight Profile - A shuttle flight, from launch to landing follows a set profile. This page outlines the main steps in a typical Space Shuttle mission.

Orbiter Vehicle (OV) - The OV consists of a pressurised cabin for crew and an un-pressurised payload bay for cargo. It also houses the main engines for ascent and decent and thruster rockets for maneuvering in orbit. It has wings and tail for aerodynamic flight during landing.

Solid Rocket Boosters (SRB) - The two SRB's provide most of the lift during the early part of flight and are attached to the sides of the External Tank. When the SRB's fuel is used up, after about two minutes into the flight, they are jettisoned and parachute back to Earth for collection and re-use.

External Tank - The External Tank holds fuel for the Orbiter's main engines and is attached to the orbiter for most of the ascent. When the tank is empty it is jettisoned and burns up during re-entry into the Earth's atmosphere.
